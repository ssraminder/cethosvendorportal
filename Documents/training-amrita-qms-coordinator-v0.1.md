## 1. Welcome and what your role does

Welcome to the team. The sprint you're joining has a hard deadline — a pharma sponsor's vendor qualification audit on June 29-30, 2026 — and a clear goal: pass that audit cleanly, and use the same work to set up Cethos's formal ISO certification later in the year. Your role is critical to both.

The QMS Coordinator owns the systematic discipline that turns operational quality work into an audit-defensible record system. Three things will keep this audit on track:

**Document control.** Every SOP, every policy, every register has a version, an author, a reviewer, an approver, an effective date, a review date, and a change history. The same document doesn't exist in three different versions in three different folders. When the auditor asks "what version of SOP-VND-001 is currently in force?" you can answer it in 30 seconds without checking with anyone.

**Records and evidence.** Every linguist in the qualified COA pool — the group of translators eligible for clinical outcome assessment work — has a complete file: identity verification, credentials with verification status, signed current NDA, training records, performance history. When the auditor asks "show me the evidence that supports this translator's qualification," you can surface it.

**Audit logistics.** When the audit happens, you make documents appear quickly and accurately when the auditor or your colleagues need them. You also keep the action tracker, the decision log, and the meeting minutes that make the whole sprint coherent.

Two important framings:

You don't need to be an ISO expert to do this role well. You need to be **systematic, organized, and willing to verify rather than assume.** The role-specific knowledge — what evidence satisfies what competence path, how SOPs should be structured, how the audit dossier should be organized — is teachable within 5 weeks.

You're not an interviewee for the audit in the way Raminder and Fayza are. The auditor may ask you to surface documents or demonstrate the document control system. They likely won't probe you on policy or methodology. **If the auditor asks you a question outside document/records territory, the right answer is: "Fayza handles that — Fayza, can you take this?"** You're allowed to redirect. You're not allowed to guess.

Five competence areas by end of Week 5:

1. **Cethos and ISO 17100 foundations** — enough understanding of what Cethos does and what ISO 17100 requires that every SOP you handle makes sense.
2. **Document control mastery** — fluent on version control, change history, three-signature workflow, the document register, the SOP template.
3. **Vendor evidence taxonomy** — given any credential, you know what it proves and where it fits in the qualification record.
4. **Records compilation and dossier work** — capable of building and maintaining the COA pool dossier and the audit dossier independently.
5. **Audit logistics command** — capable of running the virtual audit room, surfacing documents on request, and managing the action tracker during the audit itself.

## 3. ISO 17100 for non-specialists

A short orientation that will serve you for the next 5 weeks. Re-read it whenever something doesn't make sense.

### 3.1 What ISO 17100 is

ISO 17100:2015 is the international standard for Translation Service Providers. It's not about how to translate well as an individual; it's about how a translation company should be organized and operated to consistently deliver quality translation work. A company certified to ISO 17100 has demonstrated to an independent certification body that it meets every clause of the standard.

The standard is structured into eight numbered chapters. The ones that matter operationally:

- **§3 — Terms and definitions.** Defines what specific words mean. "Translator," "reviser," "revision," "review" all have specific meanings; using them loosely in SOPs is a mistake.
- **§4 — Resources.** What the company needs to have. §4.1 covers people; §4.5 covers technology.
- **§5 — Pre-production processes.** What happens before translation starts. Inquiry, quotation, client agreement, project specifications.
- **§6 — Production processes.** The actual translation work. Translation, check, revision, review, proofreading, final verification, release.
- **§7 — Post-production processes.** Feedback handling, project closure.
- **§8 — Client feedback.** How feedback is captured and used.

You don't need to memorize the standard. You need enough familiarity that when an SOP references "ISO 17100 §6.4," you know that's about revision.

### 3.2 Translator competence — the three paths

This is the single most-asked-about topic in ISO 17100 audits. The standard (§3.1.4) lists three paths a translator can meet:

- **(a)** Degree in translation from a recognized institution.
- **(b)** Degree in any other field, plus 2 years documented professional translation experience.
- **(c)** 5 years documented professional translation experience.

Path (a) is the cleanest — a single document. Path (b) needs two documents (a degree and 2 years of evidence). Path (c) needs the most documentation (5 years of evidence).

"Documented" means evidence on file, not self-attestation. A translator who says "I've been translating for 10 years" but has no documents is not qualified under §3.1.4(c).

Your job in dossier work is to capture, for each qualified linguist, which path applies and what specific documents prove it.

### 3.3 The roles ISO 17100 distinguishes

These five roles have different requirements:

- **Translator (§3.1.4).** Produces the translation. Must meet one of the three paths above.
- **Reviser (§3.1.5).** Bilingually examines the translation against the source for accuracy and quality. Must meet translator competence plus have documented revision experience plus relevant subject expertise. *Cannot be the same person as the translator on the same project.*
- **Reviewer (§3.1.6).** Monolingually examines the target text for suitability for purpose. Doesn't need to be bilingual. Required only when client agreement specifies.
- **Proofreader (§3.1.7).** Final correction pass before release. Doesn't need full translator competence.
- **Project manager (§4.1.5).** Manages projects per the SOPs.

Confusing these terms is a common SOP authorship mistake. If you read "the reviewer shall check the target against the source" — that's wrong; that's revision, not review.

### 3.4 What an auditor looks for

In broad strokes, an ISO 17100 auditor is testing whether:

- The company has the right people with documented competence
- The company has the right technology and infrastructure
- Projects flow through a defined, repeatable process
- Every required step happens and is documented
- Quality is monitored and improved over time
- Customer requirements are captured and met
- Records are maintained and retrievable

Every SOP, every register, every record exists to evidence one of those points.

## 4. Document control fundamentals

This is your home territory. Master it.

### 4.1 What document control is for

A QMS only works if everyone is using the current version of every document, knows where to find it, and can trust that records are accurate. Document control is the system that makes this true.

Three failure modes a document control system prevents:
1. **Outdated versions in use.** Someone follows SOP-VND-001 v1.0 when v2.3 is in force.
2. **Records gaps.** A required record (training completion, NDA signature, vendor evaluation) wasn't captured, so an audit can't verify the activity happened.
3. **Inconsistency.** Two SOPs say different things about the same process.

An ISO 17100 auditor will specifically test for all three.

### 4.2 The three-signature workflow

Every SOP and policy in the Cethos QMS goes through three signatures before publication:

1. **Author** — writes the document. The author confirms the content is correct.
2. **Reviewer** — checks the document against standards, related SOPs, and operational reality. For production SOPs the reviewer is typically the consultant. For management and infosec SOPs the reviewer is Fayza or Raminder.
3. **Approver** — signs off as the responsible authority. For policies (Quality Policy, Information Security Policy) the approver is Raminder. For SOPs the approver is typically Fayza.

The signatures are recorded in the document's header/signature block and reflected in the document register. **You publish a document only when all three signatures are recorded.**

If a document is needed urgently and a signature is pending, the right answer is *not* to publish without it. The right answer is to escalate to Raminder.

### 4.3 The document register

A single Google Sheet maintained by you. One row per controlled document. Columns:

| Column | Notes |
|---|---|
| Code | e.g., SOP-PROD-001 |
| Title | e.g., "Forward Translation SOP" |
| Type | SOP / Policy / Form / Register |
| Version | e.g., 1.0, 1.1, 2.0 |
| Status | draft / in_review / approved / published / superseded / withdrawn |
| Owner | role title, not personal name (e.g., "Quality Manager") |
| Author | personal name |
| Reviewer | personal name |
| Approver | personal name |
| Effective date | when this version becomes active |
| Next review date | typically effective date + 12 months |
| Last reviewed | most recent review |
| Location | Drive path |
| Supersedes | previous version code if any |
| Notes | brief notes |

The register is the auditor's navigation tool. When they ask "what version of SOP-VND-001 is in force?" you look it up in the register and answer. Don't trust file names; trust the register.

### 4.4 Version numbering

- **Drafts** are versioned 0.1, 0.2, 0.3 — anything pre-publication.
- **First published version** is 1.0.
- **Minor updates** (clarifications, formatting, non-substantive changes) advance the second number: 1.1, 1.2.
- **Major updates** (substantive changes to procedure or requirements) advance the first number: 2.0, 3.0.

Every version change requires the full three-signature workflow.

### 4.5 Change history

Every document has a change history table at the front or back. Each row records:
- Version
- Effective date
- Author
- Summary of changes
- Reason for change

This is what an auditor checks when they want to understand how a document has evolved. Don't backfill change history; record it as changes happen.

### 4.6 Naming conventions

File names follow the pattern: `[CODE]-[shortname]-[version].ext`

Examples:
- `SOP-PROD-001-forward-translation-v1.0.pdf`
- `POL-IS-001-information-security-policy-v1.0.pdf`
- `REG-VND-001-vendor-qualification-register.gsheet`

Document codes follow the pattern `[TYPE]-[AREA]-[NUMBER]`:
- **TYPE**: SOP / POL / REG / FRM / IND
- **AREA**: PROD (production), VND (vendor), MGT (management system), IS (information security), QPL (quality policy)
- **NUMBER**: zero-padded 3-digit, sequential within area

When in doubt about a code, ask Fayza or Raminder.

### 4.7 Superseded documents

When a new version of a document is published, the previous version doesn't disappear — it's marked `superseded` in the register and moved to an archive subfolder. Auditors will sometimes ask to see prior versions to understand evolution; you must be able to surface them.

Never delete a superseded document. The retention rules in SOP-MGT-002 cover how long they're kept.

## 5. SOP template and standards

You'll receive SOPs from Fayza and Raminder during the sprint, plus you'll author some yourself. Every SOP follows the same template.

### 5.1 Required template structure

(See §3 of Fayza's training plan for the full SOP authorship guide. Summary here.)

Eight sections in this order:

1. **Header** — code, title, version, effective date, review date, signatures
2. **Purpose** — one paragraph
3. **Scope** — what's in, what's out
4. **References** — standards, regulations, related SOPs
5. **Definitions** — only terms used in this SOP
6. **Responsibilities** — by role, not by name
7. **Procedure** — numbered, active voice, with actors
8. **Records** — what records are generated, where they're maintained, retention period

Plus a **change history** table at the front or back.

### 5.2 What to flag back to the author

When you receive an SOP from Fayza or Raminder for formatting, your formatting work is mechanical (apply template, adjust formatting, populate header). But you also act as a quality check; flag back to the author if you notice any of:

- The SOP references a person by name rather than role title
- The SOP references another SOP that doesn't exist in the register
- The SOP uses "must" or "will" instead of "shall" / "should" / "may"
- The SOP's Records section lists records that nobody is currently generating
- The SOP's Responsibilities section uses a role title that doesn't appear elsewhere in the QMS
- The Procedure section has steps without an actor (passive voice or floating instructions)

You don't fix these; you flag them. The author rewrites; you re-format.

### 5.3 The publication workflow

For every SOP:

1. Author submits draft (in Google Docs in `/draft-sops/`)
2. You format to template, populate header
3. Reviewer reviews and signs
4. Approver approves and signs
5. You finalize as PDF, move to the appropriate published folder, update the document register status to `published`, send the publication notification to the team

If review or approval is held up for more than 48 hours, flag to Raminder. The sprint can't tolerate documents stuck in approval queues.

## 6. Vendor evidence taxonomy

What documents prove what competence path. This is the knowledge you need for COA pool dossier work.

### 6.1 ISO 17100 §3.1.4(a) — Degree in translation

**Evidence needed:** Degree certificate, transcript, or formal letter from the issuing institution stating the qualification.

**What counts:**
- BA, MA, or PhD in Translation, Translation Studies, Translation and Interpreting, or equivalent named degree
- A specialized translation diploma or graduate certificate from a recognized post-secondary institution
- A degree in a foreign language with translation as a major component (e.g., "BA in Spanish Translation")

**What doesn't count:**
- A general foreign language degree (BA in Spanish, BA in French) without translation focus → use path (b) or (c) instead
- A short online course or certificate from a non-accredited body
- A degree from an institution we can't verify exists

**Verification methods:**
- Direct check with the institution's registrar (preferred)
- Verification through a credential evaluation service (WES, IQAS) where the linguist is foreign-credentialed
- Visual inspection of an original or notarized copy with documented review by the QA Manager

Record this in `qms.competence_evidence` with `evidence_type = degree_translation`, `verified = true`, and document the verification method.

### 6.2 ISO 17100 §3.1.4(b) — Degree in other field + 2 years documented experience

**Evidence needed:** (1) Degree certificate in any field, plus (2) at least 2 years of documented professional translation experience.

**Documented experience evidence (need at least one strong source, or multiple weaker sources):**
- Employment contract or letter from a prior translation agency or LSP showing translation work duration
- Client reference letters specifying nature and duration of translation work
- Tax records or invoices showing translation income over the period
- Project records or vendor records from a prior agency showing volume of translation work
- ATA or other professional association membership records showing tenure
- Published translations with credit lines (book translations, journal translations)

**What doesn't count as "documented":**
- Self-attestation alone
- A LinkedIn profile claiming years of experience
- A resume listing translation work without external corroboration

Note: 2 years means cumulative documented experience, not necessarily continuous.

### 6.3 ISO 17100 §3.1.4(c) — 5 years documented experience

Same evidence types as (b), but longer span. Aim for triangulated evidence — two or three sources confirming the timeline.

### 6.4 ISO 17100 §3.1.5 — Reviser

A reviser must meet **all three**:
1. Translator competence under §3.1.4 (any path) — see above
2. Documented revision experience — typically employment records, project records, or references specifying revision (not just translation) work
3. Subject matter expertise relevant to the project type

For COA pool revisers specifically, the subject matter requirement is life sciences / clinical experience.

### 6.5 Subject matter evidence (separate from role qualification)

For COA pool members, life sciences / clinical subject matter qualification requires:
- Project history showing life sciences translation work (preferred — drawn from Cethos's own project records or prior agency records)
- Or formal training (medical translation course, life sciences degree, clinical background)
- Or domain-specific certification (CTP medical specialization, ATA Medical Translation certificate, equivalent)

### 6.6 Language proficiency (separate from role qualification)

Language proficiency in working languages is part of translator and interpreter qualification. Evidence types:
- Language proficiency test results (CILISAT, ILR, ACTFL, CEFR assessment, internal Cethos test)
- Native speaker status (documented residency / education in the country of origin)
- A degree taught in the language

### 6.7 NDA / confidentiality

Every qualified linguist must have:
- Current signed NDA in `qms.nda_agreements` with `status = active`
- NDA covers data classification levels including clinical trial data
- Where the NDA has an expiry date, it's in the future
- Where the linguist is in a jurisdiction requiring it, the NDA is countersigned by Cethos

### 6.8 Training and acknowledgment

Each qualified COA pool linguist must have:
- Documented completion of COA methodology training (record from training system)
- Acknowledged the Acceptable Use Policy (signature record)
- Acknowledged data handling rules specific to clinical trial materials

## 7. COA pool dossier — what each file contains

Each linguist in the qualified COA pool has a folder at `07-vendor-dossier/[linguist_id]/`. The folder contains six standard subfolders. Your job is to ensure each contains the expected content.

### 7.1 `identity/`

- Government-issued ID document (passport preferred for foreign linguists, driver's license acceptable for Canadian)
- Contact information confirmation record (current email, phone, mailing address)
- Bank/payment information (where retained — note that this may live in a separate finance system, in which case record the reference)

### 7.2 `credentials/`

- Degree certificate(s) — original PDFs or scanned originals
- Professional translation certifications (ATA, CTTIC, ATIA, etc.) where applicable
- Specialty certifications (medical translator, etc.) where applicable
- Verification records for each credential — who verified, when, how

### 7.3 `experience/`

- Documented professional translation experience records — contracts, letters, references, tax records, prior agency records
- Project history summary (where available from Cethos's own records)
- Volume indicators (approximate words/year, projects/year)

### 7.4 `nda/`

- Current signed NDA (PDF of signed document)
- NDA history if multiple versions over time
- Countersignature record where applicable

### 7.5 `training/`

- COA methodology training completion record
- Data handling training acknowledgment
- Acceptable Use Policy acknowledgment
- Continuing professional development records where available

### 7.6 `performance/`

- Performance summary (drawn from `qms.linguist_performance_snapshot` materialized view in the database)
- Recent project records
- Customer feedback specific to this linguist
- Revision findings history
- Any complaints or commendations

### 7.7 What's "complete"

A dossier is considered complete when:
- All six subfolders have content
- Each ISO 17100 §3.1.4 path requirement has corresponding evidence with `verified = true`
- NDA is active and not expired
- Training records are current
- Performance data is current (last 12 months minimum)

When a dossier has gaps that can't be closed before audit day, document a **compensating-control narrative**: a written explanation of why the gap exists, what alternative evidence supports the qualification (e.g., extensive Cethos project history with no complaints), and what plan exists to close the gap. Save in `[linguist_id]/compensating-control-narrative.md`.

This is more defensible at audit than either (i) hiding the gap or (ii) removing the linguist from the pool without justification.

## 8. Internal Audit basics for SOP authoring

Quick orientation so the Internal Audit SOP you write reflects how internal audits actually work.

An internal audit cycle has five stages:

1. **Annual audit plan.** Top management approves which clauses, processes, and areas will be audited and when. Documented in the audit program.
2. **Audit preparation.** The auditor (internal to Cethos but independent of the area being audited) reviews relevant SOPs, prepares a checklist, and schedules the audit.
3. **Audit execution.** Opening meeting → evidence gathering through document review and interviews → closing meeting with preliminary findings. Typically one day for a focused area, two or three days for a full QMS audit.
4. **Reporting.** Findings categorized as critical / major / minor / observation. Audit report shared with auditees and top management.
5. **Follow-up.** Each finding tracked to closure with documented corrective action. Findings register maintained in the QMS system.

The Internal Audit SOP you'll write in Week 3 codifies this cycle for Cethos. Consultant supplies the template; you adapt it.

Important: The auditor at the late-June pharma sponsor's audit may ask whether Cethos has performed an internal audit recently. If we haven't, the honest answer is *"We're in QMS build-out for ISO certification; our first formal internal audit is scheduled for Q4 2026 in preparation for Orion certification."* That's a legitimate position. Don't manufacture a fake internal audit. The Internal Audit SOP that *will* govern audits going forward is itself audit evidence.

## 9. Information security SOPs — what each one covers

You'll author five infosec SOPs in Week 4. Here's what each one is for, so the writing flows.

### 9.1 Access Control SOP

Who can access what systems, what data, at what level. Includes:
- Role-based access principle
- Authentication requirements (passwords, MFA)
- Access provisioning workflow (request → approval → provisioning)
- Access review cadence (typically quarterly)
- Access revocation on staff or linguist offboarding

### 9.2 Encryption and Secure Transmission SOP

How information is protected in transit and at rest. Includes:
- Encryption in transit: TLS for web, encrypted email or secure portal for sensitive files
- Encryption at rest: database encryption, cloud storage encryption, device disk encryption
- Specific prohibition on email attachments of clinical trial source materials
- Use of secure portals for clinical trial document exchange

### 9.3 Backup and Business Continuity SOP

How Cethos protects against data loss and operational disruption. Includes:
- Backup cadence and retention
- Backup testing cadence (typically quarterly)
- Recovery objectives (RTO, RPO) for critical systems
- Business continuity plan for major disruption scenarios

### 9.4 Device Security SOP

How devices used to access Cethos systems and data are secured. Includes:
- Approved device types and operating systems
- Required device security configuration (full disk encryption, screen lock, automatic updates, antivirus where applicable)
- Removable media policy
- Lost or compromised device procedure (referenced in Incident Response)

### 9.5 Data Retention and Secure Disposal SOP

How data is retained and how it's disposed of. Includes:
- Retention periods by data classification
- Clinical trial data retention per sponsor agreement (typically aligned to GCP — 15-25 years)
- Secure disposal methods (cryptographic erasure, certified destruction)
- Disposal records

These SOPs don't require deep operational knowledge of Cethos — they describe how things *should* be done, aligned to industry norms. The consultant supplies templates; you adapt them. Where you don't know how Cethos currently does something, ask Fayza.
