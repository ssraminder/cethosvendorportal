# Fayza — Training Plan
## Quality Manager (Operational) — 5-Week Audit-Readiness Sprint

**Document status:** v0.1 — for use during sprint May 11 → June 12, 2026
**Audit role:** Quality Manager — primary interviewee on every operational topic
**Estimated study time:** 35-40 hours over 5 weeks (in addition to sprint work)

---

## 1. Why your role matters most

The auditor will spend the majority of the two audit days on operational topics: vendor qualification, COA methodology, translation production workflow, revision controls, complaint handling, training records. **You are the primary interviewee for every one of those.** Raminder handles the strategic and policy questions. Amrita handles document retrieval. You answer the operational question.

This is a step up from translator scouting and vendor relations work. The skills you've built doing that work are exactly the foundation needed — you know the linguists, you understand the production reality, you've seen what works and what fails in the actual delivery of translation services. The training is about giving that operational knowledge a formal structure and a formal vocabulary that an ISO auditor recognizes.

Three things distinguish a strong Quality Manager from someone who just answers questions:

1. **Fluency in every SOP**, even the ones you didn't author. If the auditor asks you to explain the Records Retention SOP and Amrita wrote it, you can still explain it.
2. **Evidence-first answers.** When the auditor asks "how do you do X?" your answer references the specific SOP, the specific record type, and where that record lives. Not "we usually" — "the SOP X-001 specifies, and the records are maintained in [system]."
3. **Calm under probing.** Auditors test answers by repeating the question with slight variations, or by asking for evidence right after you make a claim. The right response to "show me an example" is to surface it, not to qualify or hedge.

Five competence areas by end of Week 5:

1. **ISO 17100 fluency** — every clause, with particular depth on §3 (terms), §4 (resources), §5 (pre-production), §6 (production), §7 (post-production).
2. **COA / Linguistic Validation expert command** — ISPOR good practices, Wild et al. principles, FDA PRO Guidance, EMA reflection paper, the full forward → back → reconciliation → cognitive debriefing → harmonization cycle.
3. **Vendor qualification mastery** — given any candidate, you can map their evidence to the correct competence basis, identify gaps, and articulate the qualification decision.
4. **SOP authorship craft** — every SOP you write conforms to template, uses correct ISO language ("shall," "should," "may"), references applicable clauses, integrates with related SOPs without contradiction.
5. **Audit interview command** — you drive operational sessions; the auditor leaves understanding the system because you've explained it clearly.

## 3. SOP authorship guide

Every SOP you write should conform to the same structure. Memorize this; don't reinvent it per SOP.

### 3.1 Required structure

Eight sections in this order:

1. **Header.** Document code (e.g., `SOP-PROD-001`), title, version, effective date, review date, author, reviewer, approver. Amrita maintains the header template; you fill in author and content.

2. **Purpose.** One short paragraph. What this SOP exists to ensure. Example: *"This SOP defines the forward translation process for ISO 17100-scoped projects, ensuring that translations meet the competence and process requirements of ISO 17100 §6.2 and the specific methodology requirements for clinical outcome assessment work."*

3. **Scope.** What's in, what's out. Example: *"This SOP applies to all ISO 17100-scoped projects, including but not limited to certified translation, life sciences translation, and linguistic validation of clinical outcome assessments. It does not cover machine translation post-editing, which is governed by SOP-PROD-010."*

4. **References.** Standards, regulations, related SOPs. Example: *"ISO 17100:2015 §6.2; ISPOR Good Practice (Wild et al. 2005); SOP-VND-001 Translator Qualification; SOP-PROD-003 Reconciliation."*

5. **Definitions.** Only terms used in this SOP that need clarifying. Don't repeat ISO 17100 §3 wholesale; reference it.

6. **Responsibilities.** Who does what. Use role titles, not personal names. *"Project Manager," "Translator," "Reviser," "Quality Manager."*

7. **Procedure.** The actual step-by-step. Numbered. Each step active voice. *"7.1 The Project Manager receives the source material from the client..."*

8. **Records.** What records are generated by this procedure, where they're maintained, retention period. *"Translation memory updates retained in [system] for [period]. Project specifications retained per SOP-MGT-002 Records Retention."*

### 3.2 ISO language conventions

Three verbs matter. Use them deliberately.

- **shall** — mandatory requirement. *"The translator shall meet ISO 17100 §3.1.4 competence requirements."* Auditors will hold you to every "shall."
- **should** — recommended; deviation is acceptable with documented rationale. *"The reviser should normally not be the same person as the reviewer."*
- **may** — permitted; optional. *"The Project Manager may delegate intake confirmation to the Project Coordinator."*

Avoid: "will," "must," "is required to," "needs to" — these are colloquial and ambiguous in auditor reading. Stick to shall/should/may.

### 3.3 Reference convention

When you reference another SOP, ISO clause, or external document, use the formal code. *"per SOP-VND-001 §4.2"* — not "per the vendor qualification procedure." This makes the document register navigable and the audit dossier searchable.

### 3.4 Top 5 SOP authorship mistakes to avoid

1. **Mixing "shall" and "should" inconsistently.** If forward translation by a qualified translator is mandatory, write "shall." Don't soften it to "should" because it sounds gentler.

2. **Procedure steps that aren't actually steps.** Each numbered step should be an action with an actor. *"The translator shall produce the target text"* — yes. *"Quality is important"* — not a step.

3. **References to people, departments, or systems that no longer exist.** Every SOP gets a name/system pass before publication. If you reference "Maria" in any SOP, fix it.

4. **Procedure that contradicts another SOP.** If SOP-PROD-001 says revision happens after translation and SOP-VND-001 says revisers are assigned at project start, that's a contradiction the auditor will catch. Cross-reference and harmonize.

5. **Records section that lists records nobody actually generates.** If the SOP says "the translator records time spent per segment" and no translator does that, the auditor will find the gap. Records sections must reflect reality.

## 4. Vendor qualification decision guide

The mental model for taking a candidate and arriving at a defensible qualification decision.

### 4.1 Translator qualification — three paths

For any candidate seeking translator qualification on the ISO 17100-scoped pool, work through the paths in order. Take the first one that's satisfied.

**Path (a): Recognized degree in translation.** §3.1.4(a). Required evidence: degree certificate from a recognized post-secondary institution in translation, translation studies, or equivalent. Verified by direct check with the issuing institution where possible, or by documented review of the certificate. Recorded as evidence type `degree_translation`.

**Path (b): Recognized degree in any field + 2 years documented professional translation experience.** §3.1.4(b). Required evidence: degree certificate (any field) plus documented evidence of at least 2 years professional translation experience. Documented experience means: employment contracts, client references, tax records showing translation income, project records from prior agencies. "Self-attestation only" is insufficient. Recorded as evidence types `degree_other` + `documented_translation_experience`.

**Path (c): 5 years documented professional translation experience.** §3.1.4(c). Required evidence: documented evidence of at least 5 years professional translation experience (same evidence types as path b, but longer span). Recorded as evidence type `documented_translation_experience`.

If none of these can be satisfied with documented evidence, the candidate **is not qualified** under ISO 17100 §3.1.4 and may not be assigned to ISO-scoped projects. Discuss with the Head of Quality; they may still be qualified for non-ISO work, but that's outside our scope.

### 4.2 Reviser qualification (§3.1.5)

A reviser must meet **all three**:
1. Translator competence under §3.1.4 (any of the three paths above)
2. Documented revision experience
3. Subject matter expertise relevant to the projects they revise

For the COA pool specifically, the subject matter requirement means demonstrable life sciences / clinical experience — not just life sciences translation but understanding of the subject matter sufficient to catch substantive errors.

### 4.3 Post-editor qualification (ISO 18587 §3.1)

A post-editor must meet:
1. Translator competence under ISO 17100 §3.1.4
2. PEMT training or documented experience

Note: PEMT is typically excluded from COA work. Document this position in the COA Methodology Master Document.

### 4.4 Interpreter qualification (ISO 18841 §6)

An interpreter must meet **one of**:
1. Recognized interpreter training certificate plus documented language proficiency in the working languages
2. Alternative: 5 years documented professional interpretation experience

Plus mode-specific qualification (consecutive / simultaneous / OPI / VRI) where applicable. Plus NSGCIS-specific cultural competence and ethics training for community interpreting.

### 4.5 Subject matter qualification (separate from role qualification)

For COA pool translators, subject matter qualification in life sciences / clinical requires:
- Documented experience translating life sciences content (project history acceptable as evidence)
- Or formal training (medical translation course, life sciences degree, clinical background)
- Or domain-specific certification (CTP medical specialization, ATA Medical Translation certificate, equivalent)

Document the basis and the evidence. The auditor will ask why a translator is qualified for COA work specifically, not just for translation generally.

### 4.6 NDA and information security

Every qualified linguist must have:
- A signed current NDA on file (status `active` in the QMS database)
- Documented training on data handling (specifically clinical trial data classification and handling)
- Acknowledged the Acceptable Use Policy

A linguist with active NDA expired or pending = not eligible for assignment. The QMS gating logic enforces this; you maintain it.

### 4.7 The "evidence-first" answer

When the auditor asks about a specific linguist, structure your answer in this exact order:

1. **Name and current status.** *"[Name], currently qualified active, qualified as translator for [pair], subject matter life sciences."*
2. **Competence basis.** *"Qualified under ISO 17100 §3.1.4(a) — degree in translation."*
3. **Evidence.** *"Degree certificate from [institution], verified by [method], on file as evidence record [ID]."*
4. **NDA status.** *"Current NDA signed [date], status active."*
5. **Re-qualification date.** *"Next re-qualification due [date]."*

Practice this until it flows. The auditor sees a system, not improvisation.

## 5. Operational interview Q&A bank

Twenty-five operational questions a pharma vendor QA auditor or Orion auditor will ask. Memorize the *shape* and the *facts*. Practice delivering each in your own words.

### Vendor qualification (the hottest topic)

**Q1. Walk me through how Cethos qualifies a translator.**
> Our forward-going qualification pipeline is the Cethos Vetting Program, or CVP. A candidate applies; the application is captured with role type, education, certifications, services offered, work samples; AI prescreening produces an initial score and surfaces flags for staff review. If the candidate passes prescreening, they're assigned tests from our test library, matched to their language pairs and domains. Reference checks happen in parallel. The decision step combines test results, references, and prescreening evidence. If approved, the candidate is onboarded — NDA signed, training assigned, system access granted. They're added to the qualified vendor pool with their approved language pair and domain combinations. We then monitor performance continuously through revision findings, on-time delivery, and customer feedback. Re-qualification occurs annually as a minimum, or earlier if performance signals warrant.

**Q2. Show me an example of a qualified translator's record.**
> [Pull up the qms.role_qualifications query for a specific COA pool member. Walk through: name, role, competence basis, evidence, language pairs, subject matter, NDA, re-qualification date. Have Amrita ready to surface evidence files.]

**Q3. How do you handle ISO 17100 §3.1.4 competence verification?**
> [Deliver §4.7 of this guide — the evidence-first answer structure.]

**Q4. Have you ever rejected a translator candidate? Why?**
> Yes. [Have a specific example ready — anonymized but real. Common pattern: "A candidate applied last [period] with self-attested 5 years experience but couldn't produce documented evidence — no contracts, no client references, no tax records substantiating the experience claim. We rejected the application with documented reasoning. Rejection is logged in cvp_application_decisions with the decision rationale."]

**Q5. What's the difference between a qualified translator and one who's just experienced?**
> ISO 17100 §3.1.4 distinguishes between professional translation experience and documented professional translation experience. A translator who has been working for ten years but cannot produce evidence of that experience is not qualified under §3.1.4(c). Qualification requires evidence — degree certificate, employment records, client references, tax records. Self-attestation alone is not sufficient.

**Q6. How is your qualified COA pool defined and maintained?**
> The qualified COA pool is a subset of our overall qualified translator pool meeting three additional criteria: documented life sciences subject matter expertise, completion of our COA methodology training, and active NDA covering clinical trial data classification. Membership is maintained by performance monitoring; if a translator on the pool has revision findings above threshold, a complaint, or fails re-qualification, they're removed pending review. The pool composition is reviewed monthly.

### Production process (ISO 17100 §6)

**Q7. Walk me through your forward translation process.**
> [Reference SOP-PROD-001 Forward Translation. Walk through: project receipt by qualified translator; source review; translation production using approved CAT tool; self-check per §6.3; submission to revision per §6.4. Each step has records — translator assignment record in our project system, CAT tool packages, translator notes.]

**Q8. How do you ensure revision is performed by a different person than the translator?**
> Workflow enforcement in our project system. The translator assignment and the reviser assignment are separate records, and the system gates assignment such that the same person cannot be assigned to both roles on the same project. SOP-PROD-002 Revision specifies the §6.4 requirement explicitly. The record showing translator A → reviser B for any given project is retrievable.

**Q9. What's your back translation methodology?**
> [Reference SOP-PROD-002 Back Translation. Walk through: independent back translator, not exposed to original source; back translation against finalized forward translation; reconciliation against source to identify conceptual mismatches; reconciliation report. Aligned to ISPOR Wild et al. principles.]

**Q10. How do you handle harmonization in multi-language COA projects?**
> [Reference SOP-PROD-005 Harmonization. Harmonization meeting with linguists from each language version, source author or sponsor representative, methodology lead. Output: harmonization minutes documenting decisions on conceptual equivalence across languages, with rationale for any deviations. Stored in project records.]

**Q11. What records do you keep for a typical COA project?**
> Project specification (client-agreed), translator and reviser assignment records, forward translation files, revision findings, reconciliation against source, back translation files, back translation comparison, cognitive debriefing report (where applicable), harmonization meeting minutes (where applicable), final QC report, client acceptance record, project closure record with retention plan. All retained per SOP-MGT-002 Records Retention.

### Training and competence

**Q12. What training do COA translators receive?**
> COA methodology training covering the ISPOR good practice principles, our internal procedures for forward/back/reconciliation/cognitive debriefing/harmonization, and data handling for clinical trial materials. Training records are maintained per translator with completion dates and competence assessment. Re-training is triggered by methodology updates or performance signals.

**Q13. How do you assess training effectiveness?**
> Through performance monitoring — translators who've completed COA training are tracked against project quality metrics. Revision findings, on-time delivery, customer feedback are the primary indicators. Training that doesn't translate into operational quality triggers review of the training itself.

### Information security (operational level)

**Q14. How does a clinical trial source document move from sponsor to translator?**
> Sponsor delivers source materials through their secure portal or our secure project portal. The Project Manager downloads to our access-controlled project storage. The qualified translator receives access via our project system — no email attachments of source content for clinical trial materials. The translator works in their approved CAT tool environment, which holds the source and target text in an encrypted package. On project closure, source materials are returned or destroyed per sponsor agreement, and the destruction or return is documented.

**Q15. What happens if a linguist's device is lost or compromised?**
> [Reference SOP-IS-005 Incident Response. The linguist reports immediately per their Acceptable Use Policy; the Project Manager and Quality Manager are notified; access is revoked from project systems within agreed time; the incident is logged; if clinical trial data is involved, sponsor notification occurs per agreed timelines; root cause analysis and corrective action follow.]

**Q16. How do you control subprocessor data handling?**
> Every linguist signs an NDA before any project work. The NDA includes specific provisions on clinical trial data classification, no transmission to third parties, no retention beyond project requirements, no use for any purpose other than the assigned project. The Acceptable Use Policy reinforces the contractual position. Compliance is monitored through access logs and project audit.

### Quality and improvement

**Q17. How is a customer complaint handled?**
> [Reference SOP-MGT-006 Customer Complaint Handling. Receipt — any channel logged to register within 24 hours. Acknowledgment to customer within 48 hours. Root cause investigation by Quality Manager with assigned timeline. Corrective action and where appropriate preventive action defined. Customer response with outcome. Closure logged. Complaints above defined severity threshold trigger CAPA process per SOP-MGT-007.]

**Q18. Show me a complaint that was handled recently.**
> [Have a specific anonymized example ready. Pull the register entry; walk through receipt, investigation, action, closure. If complaint volume is low, that itself is part of the answer — but with evidence.]

**Q19. How do you measure on-time delivery?**
> Per project, agreed delivery date vs actual delivery date is captured. Aggregated by month and reported in management review. Late delivery above threshold triggers root cause review of the project — was it specification, capacity, vendor issue, internal error? — and where the root cause is systematic, CAPA.

**Q20. What's your highest-impact recent quality improvement?**
> [Have a real example. Likely candidates: introduction of CVP as the formal qualification pipeline; the structured vendor qualification database replacing spreadsheet records; the COA methodology master document consolidating prior fragmented procedures; the integrated ISO certification effort itself.]

### Records and document control

**Q21. How do you maintain document control?**
> [Reference SOP-MGT-001 Document Control. Single document register maintained by the QMS Coordinator. Every SOP has version, effective date, review date, author, reviewer, approver. Change history captured. Three-signature workflow enforces author/reviewer/approver. Obsolete versions retained but clearly marked. Amrita can demonstrate the system.]

**Q22. How long do you retain project records?**
> [Reference SOP-MGT-002 Records Retention. Default: 7 years for project records, longer where sponsor agreement specifies. Clinical trial project records typically retained per sponsor's GCP retention requirements — 15 to 25 years depending on jurisdiction and study type. Specific retention is captured per project at intake.]

**Q23. How are records protected against unauthorized change or deletion?**
> Records in the QMS database are append-only for audit logs at the database level — no updates or deletions are permitted at all for the qualification audit log table; this is enforced by database permission grants, not by convention. Other records have role-based access control with audit logging on changes. Physical or PDF records are stored in access-controlled cloud storage with versioning and audit logging.
