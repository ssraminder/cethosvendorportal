## 1. What you need to know by Audit Day

You already know Cethos better than anyone. The audit work is not learning the business — it's learning the **vocabulary and structure of formal quality management** so that what you've actually been doing for ten years appears, on the day, as a deliberate ISO-aligned system rather than improvised operational competence.

Five competence areas by end of Week 5:

1. **ISO 9001 leadership clauses (§4-§5)** — fluent on what "top management" means in ISO terms, what the audit will probe at the executive level, how to articulate Cethos's strategic context and quality commitment.
2. **ISO 17100 strategic-level requirements** — fluent on the certificate scope, the standard's structure, what it requires of an organization (vs what it requires of individual translators).
3. **Information security executive posture** — fluent on the Cethos ISMS-lite at policy level, the data classification scheme, the subprocessor framework, the position on 21 CFR Part 11 and GDPR.
4. **Executive audit interview craft** — able to handle the auditor's opening interview, organizational-context questions, and closing-meeting findings negotiation without contradiction or hedging.
5. **Audit-day strategic command** — able to direct the team in real-time during the audit, handle surprises, and protect the organization's interests during closing.

You are not expected to be fluent on every SOP at the level Fayza is. The auditor will *ask* you policy and strategic questions and *probe* Fayza on operational and methodology questions. When operational topics come to you, the correct answer is to acknowledge them and redirect: *"Fayza owns that operationally — Fayza, can you take this?"*

## 2. Curriculum by week

### Week 1 — May 11-15. Strategic foundation

**Topics:** ISO 9001 leadership clauses + your role as top management + organizational context. Approximately 3 hours of focused study; spread across the week as fits the sprint schedule.

**Required reading.**
- ISO 9001:2015 §4 (Context of the organization) — full text, ~30 min. Pay particular attention to §4.1 (understanding the organization and its context), §4.2 (needs and expectations of interested parties), §4.3 (scope of the QMS).
- ISO 9001:2015 §5 (Leadership) — full text, ~45 min. Pay particular attention to §5.1 (leadership and commitment), §5.2 (policy), §5.3 (organizational roles, responsibilities, authorities).
- ISO 17100:2015 §1-§3 — scope, normative references, terms and definitions. ~30 min.
- The Cethos certificate scope statement (from the Orion application). ~5 min. Memorize this.

**Practical exercises.**
- Draft the Quality Policy v1 in Week 1 (sprint task). The act of writing it is the training. It should make explicit reference to: customer focus, ISO 17100 + 9001 + 18587 + 18841 + NSGCIS conformance commitment, continual improvement, customer-defined specifications (not internally-designed services — this anchors the §8.3 exclusion).
- Memorize the certificate scope statement word-for-word. The auditor *will* ask you what the scope of certification activity is and you should be able to recite it.
- Prepare a 90-second verbal answer to: *"Tell me about Cethos — when was it founded, what does it do, who are your customers, where are you in your quality journey?"* Practice until it flows.

**Self-check by end of Week 1.**
- I can name the five standards in the integrated audit and the one ISO 9001 exclusion (§8.3 Design and Development) without looking.
- I can explain in two sentences why Cethos qualifies for the §8.3 exclusion: *"We deliver services based on client-provided source materials and client-defined specifications. We do not design the translation product itself — the source and specifications are the customer's."*
- I can name the four "interested parties" relevant to Cethos's QMS without looking: customers (especially life sciences sponsors), translators and interpreters (external resources), regulatory bodies (IRCC, FDA via clinical sponsors), employees.

### Week 2 — May 18-22. ISO 17100 strategic depth + scope command

**Topics:** ISO 17100 organizational requirements (vs translator-level requirements); scope of certification activity; client agreement and project specification at strategic level. Approximately 4 hours.

**Self-check by end of Week 2.**

## 3. Executive interview Q&A bank

Twenty questions a pharma vendor QA auditor or Orion auditor will likely ask the Head of Quality during the opening interview or executive segment. Model answers below. Memorize the *shape* and the *facts*, not the verbatim wording — the auditor wants natural delivery, not recitation.

### Organizational and strategic

**Q1. Tell me about Cethos — history, services, customer base.**
> Cethos Solutions was incorporated in 2019, but I started the business in 2015. We're headquartered in Calgary, Alberta. We provide translation, post-editing of machine translation, and interpretation services to clients in Canada and globally. Our work covers certified translation for immigration and legal contexts, life sciences translation including clinical outcome assessments and regulatory work, business translation, and community interpretation in 95+ languages. Our customer base spans pharma sponsors, Canadian government, immigration clients, and Canadian and international businesses. We're a BBB-accredited business with an A+ rating since 2020 and currently pursuing integrated ISO 9001, 17100, 18587, 18841, and NSGCIS certification.

**Q2. What is the scope of your quality management system?**
> [Recite the certificate scope statement word-for-word.] Provision of translation, post-editing of machine translation, and interpretation services for clients in Canada and globally, including certified translation, life sciences translation, business translation, and community interpretation, supported by project management, vendor qualification, and quality assurance processes.

**Q3. Are there any exclusions from ISO 9001?**
> One exclusion: Clause 8.3, Design and Development. Cethos delivers services based on client-provided source materials and client-defined specifications. We do not design the translation product itself; the source and the specifications come from the customer.

**Q4. Who reports to whom on quality matters?**
> I am the Head of Quality and the owner. Fayza is the Quality Manager, responsible for operational quality. Amrita is the QMS Coordinator, responsible for document control and records management. We engage an external QA Advisor for periodic independent review and audit support. The Quality Manager has the authority to halt any project that doesn't meet specification.

**Q5. How does top management demonstrate commitment to quality?**
> Three concrete ways. First, I personally signed the Quality Policy and the Information Security Policy and I'm accountable for both. Second, the QMS is resourced — we have dedicated QMS roles and budget for external advisory. Third, quality metrics are reviewed in management review at least annually and inform business decisions including vendor pool composition, training investment, and process changes. [If asked for evidence: management review minutes from the most recent cycle.]

**Q6. What is the strategic context of Cethos's QMS?**
> Two contexts shape the QMS. The first is the regulated nature of our customers — life sciences sponsors and government clients require documented quality controls and vendor qualification. The second is our reliance on a distributed network of external linguists, which means our quality system must focus heavily on vendor qualification and control. The QMS is built around those two realities.

**Q7. How has the QMS evolved recently?**
> Three changes in the past 12 months. First, we migrated from an external commercial TMS to an internal Supabase-based vendor and project management system, which lets us structure vendor qualification records as queryable data rather than spreadsheet entries. Second, we formalized the Cethos Vetting Program as our forward-going translator qualification pipeline. Third, we adjusted leadership in the QMS function — Fayza moved into the Quality Manager role and we engaged external QA advisory support, both reflecting the increased rigor required for ISO certification and life sciences sponsor work.

### Vendor and resource controls (typically deflected to Fayza after framing)

**Q8. How does Cethos qualify external linguists?**
> At a strategic level, we operate two qualification pathways. The forward-going pathway is the Cethos Vetting Program — applications, AI prescreening, references, language and domain testing, decision, training, and ongoing performance monitoring. For linguists already in our network from prior work, we apply ISO 17100 §3.1.4 competence evaluation against the same evidence standards. Fayza can walk you through the operational detail.

**Q9. How do you ensure translators are competent for life sciences / COA work?**
> Specialized subject-matter qualification beyond generic translation competence. Our qualified COA pool — translators eligible for clinical outcome assessment work — meets ISO 17100 §3.1.4 plus documented life sciences subject matter experience plus COA methodology training. Fayza maintains the pool and the documentation, and can walk you through the pool composition.

### Information security (typically deflected to consultant or framed strategically)

**Q10. How does Cethos protect client information?**
> We operate an information security framework aligned to ISO 27001 principles, though not yet certified to 27001. The framework covers data classification, access control, encryption in transit and at rest, subprocessor controls — meaning every linguist signs an NDA and operates under documented data handling rules — incident response, and backup. Clinical trial materials are classified as restricted, which triggers the strictest access and handling controls. The Information Security Policy is part of the audit dossier.

**Q11. What's your position on 21 CFR Part 11?**
> Cethos does not currently host or operate ePRO platforms or maintain electronic records that are themselves the regulatory submission. Our role produces translated content that is then incorporated into the sponsor's Part 11-compliant systems. Where Cethos handles content within a sponsor's Part 11-controlled environment, we follow the sponsor's procedures.

**Q12. What's your position on GDPR?**
> We do not currently process identifiable patient data in our linguistic validation work — source materials are typically the instrument itself, not patient responses. Where Cethos is engaged as a data processor for any sponsor activity involving EU data subjects, we operate under the sponsor's DPA.

### Quality outcomes and improvement

**Q13. How do you measure quality?**
> Multiple indicators. On-time delivery rate, revision findings per project, client satisfaction surveys, customer complaint rate, and CAPA closure rate. These feed into vendor performance records and into management review. The Quality Manager reports on these monthly.

**Q14. Tell me about a recent quality issue and how it was handled.**
> [Have one ready. A specific complaint that came in, how it was logged, root cause, corrective action, preventive action, closure. Real example; don't fabricate. If you genuinely can't think of one, say so honestly: "Let me think through that — the most recent significant case was..."]

**Q15. How do you handle customer complaints?**
> Customer complaints are received through any channel — email, phone, the project manager — and logged in the customer complaint register the same day. The Quality Manager assigns root cause investigation, defines corrective action and preventive action, and reviews closure. Complaints above a defined severity threshold trigger CAPA. The register and the SOP are in the audit dossier.

**Q16. How often do you conduct internal audits?**
> Annually as a minimum, more frequently for specific high-risk areas. The internal audit program covers all clauses of all standards in our integrated QMS scope, with findings categorized and tracked to closure. The next internal audit is scheduled for Q4 2026 in preparation for the Orion certification audit. [If pressed on whether one has been done recently: be honest. If not yet, the answer is "we are in QMS build-out for ISO certification; the first formal internal audit is scheduled for Q4 2026."]

### Strategic and forward-looking

**Q17. What's your continual improvement focus?**
> Three current priorities. First, completing ISO certification across the integrated standards bundle. Second, expanding the qualified COA translator pool to support growing life sciences demand. Third, formalizing our information security posture toward full ISO 27001 alignment.

**Q18. What risks do you see in your operating model?**
> Three. First, distributed linguist workforce — mitigated by vendor qualification, training, and performance monitoring. Second, data sensitivity in life sciences work — mitigated by the ISMS controls. Third, scaling qualified specialist capacity for COA work — mitigated by the CVP pipeline and ongoing training investment.

**Q19. Where do you want Cethos to be in three years?**
> A trusted certified specialist provider for life sciences linguistic services — translation, COA linguistic validation, and interpretation — with full ISO 17100 + 9001 + 18587 + 18841 + NSGCIS certification, ISO 27001 alignment, and a qualified COA pool of [target number] translators across the major language pairs our sponsor mix requires.

**Q20. Is there anything we haven't covered that you want us to know?**
> [Use this. The auditor offers this question because they want to know what you think is important. Use it to surface one or two positives that may not have come up: e.g., the CVP system as a forward-going qualification pipeline; the Supabase-based vendor database as a structured queryable record system; the BBB A+ rating; the fact that you've engaged external QA advisory specifically for this audit and the ISO certification, demonstrating investment in quality.]

## 4. Top 12 things NOT to say in an audit

These are the executive-level failure modes. Read this twice. Discuss with the consultant.

1. **"We always..." or "We never..."** — auditors hear these as red flags inviting them to find the counter-example. Replace with: "Our standard procedure is..." or "It's not typical for us to..."

2. **"I think we do that, let me check."** — fine if followed by actually checking. The failure mode is when you guess to fill the silence and the answer turns out wrong. Always prefer "let me check" or "Fayza would have the operational detail" to a wrong guess.

3. **"That's not really documented but we do it."** — every time this is said, the auditor logs an opportunity for a finding on document control. Better formulation: "That's currently handled through team knowledge and our [X] process; one of the items we identified for the QMS build is formalizing this in [Y SOP]."

4. **"We don't have time / resources / capacity to do that properly."** — never. Replace with: "Our approach to that is..." even if the approach is minimal. Honesty about limitations is fine; performative complaining is not.

5. **"Our previous Quality Manager [Maria] used to handle that."** — never invoke departed staff by name as the keeper of knowledge. The auditor will hear: institutional knowledge walked out the door. Replace with: "That responsibility now sits with Fayza" or "That's documented in [SOP]."

6. **"We've always passed audits before."** — irrelevant and slightly defensive. Replace with: "We take audit feedback seriously and use it to drive improvement."

7. **"That's the consultant's area."** — never deflect to the consultant in front of the auditor. The consultant is QA Advisor, not the Cethos QMS owner. You and Fayza are the owners; the consultant is support. If you genuinely need to bring the consultant in, do it with: "Our QA Advisor has supported us in developing this, but the responsibility sits with us — Fayza, can you walk through the operational detail?"

8. **Contradicting Fayza or Amrita mid-audit.** — if you hear them say something you think is wrong, do not correct them in front of the auditor. Wait for the break, clarify privately, return and offer a clarification: "I want to add one point to what Fayza shared earlier..."

9. **Volunteering information not asked for.** — answer the question. Don't expand into "and another thing..." or "we also had a situation where..." Every additional fact is a new opportunity for follow-up. Discipline matters.

10. **"We don't have any findings / issues / problems."** — auditors hear this as defensive or naïve. Every organization has quality issues; the question is whether you handle them well. Replace with: "Our CAPA log shows [X] open items currently, all within their target closure timeframes."

11. **"The standard doesn't require that."** — never. Even if true, it sounds defensive and adversarial. Replace with: "Our approach to that goes beyond / aligns with the standard's requirement by..."

12. **Over-promising remediation in the closing meeting.** — if the auditor identifies a finding and asks how you'll address it, do not commit to a timeline or specific action in the room. Replace with: "We'll review the finding, conduct root cause analysis, and respond formally within the response window — typically 30 days for the corrective action plan."

## 5. Audit-day strategic command

### 5.1 Opening meeting

You run this. Approximately 30 minutes. Agenda:

1. Welcome and introductions (Cethos team: you, Fayza, Amrita, consultant; auditor team: name, role, organization)
2. Confirm audit scope and objectives (the auditor states them; you confirm understanding)
3. Confirm logistics: virtual room, document presentation method, recording (if any), break schedule, closing meeting time
4. Confirm document index has been received and is being referenced
5. Brief organizational overview from you (3-4 minutes; Q1 in §3 expanded slightly)
6. Confirm team availability and communication protocol during the audit
7. Hand over to the auditor to drive the document review and interview agenda

Tone: professional, prepared, confident, not deferential. The auditor is a peer doing their job. You are a peer hosting them.

### 5.2 During the audit

Your role across the two days:

- **Sit in on all sessions if possible.** You'll catch issues in real-time and be ready for unexpected handoffs.
- **Direct handoffs.** When the auditor's question is operational, direct: "Fayza, can you take that?" Don't let the auditor wander.
- **Take notes.** Every question that requires follow-up, every commitment made by anyone in the room, every finding signaled by the auditor's tone — note it. Compare notes with Fayza and Amrita between sessions.
- **Between-session briefs.** During every break, the four of you (you, Fayza, Amrita, consultant) huddle for 5 minutes. What happened, what's coming, anything to adjust.
- **Don't argue findings in the moment.** If the auditor says "this looks like a gap," do not defend in the room. Acknowledge: "We'll review that and ensure it's addressed in our response." Find space later to negotiate if appropriate.

### 5.3 Closing meeting

You run this jointly with the auditor. Approximately 60-90 minutes.

The auditor presents preliminary findings — categorized as critical / major / minor / observation. Your role:

- **Listen completely before responding.** Take notes. Confirm you understand each finding.
- **Negotiate categorization where appropriate.** If a finding is categorized as Major but is genuinely Minor in your view, raise it — calmly, with specific reference to evidence. Don't fight; offer a different framing.
- **Confirm response timeline.** Typically 30 days for the corrective action plan. Confirm it.
- **Thank the auditor.** Genuine, not performative. They've done their job.
- **Close with a forward-looking statement.** "We appreciate the thorough review. We'll have the corrective action plan back to you within 30 days, and we look forward to demonstrating closure of these findings."

### 5.4 Post-audit (Week of June 30 +)

Within 48 hours of the audit closing:

- Internal debrief with the full team and consultant. What went well, what didn't, key findings, key positives.
- Findings register populated in the QMS system with owner, target date, response strategy.
- Begin drafting the corrective action plan.

Within 14 days of the audit closing:

- Corrective action plan drafted and reviewed by consultant.
- Where findings have rapid fixes, implement them and document.

Within 30 days:

- Corrective action plan submitted to the auditor.
- Track to closure.
